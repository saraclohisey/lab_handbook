Nature Masterclass – Peer Review 

# Full read through/first impressions

Here is a checklist of questions you should ensure you answer as you read through the paper for the first time:
- [ ] Is there a clear and important research question?
- [ ] Is the question properly answered by the study?
- [ ] Are all the claims supported by data?
- [ ] Is the novelty level of the study appropriate for the journal?
- [ ] Who would be interested in reading this study?
- [ ] Is the study design appropriate?
- [ ] Are there any fundamental flaws in the study design?
- [ ] Does the paper have strong technical rigour?
Useful questions to ask about the title of a paper include:
- [ ] Is the title descriptive of the report?
- [ ] Does it draw the interest of the reader?
- [ ] Does it focus on the main and most novel findings?
- [ ] Does it contain any unnecessary hype?
The abstract provides a concise overview of the study: why it was undertaken, the methods used, a summary of the findings, and an indication of any implications. It shouldn’t focus solely on the findings, and should always be academic in tone
A strong abstract will effectively summarise the paper and aim to answer five key questions about the study:
- [ ] Why did the authors carry out the study? (“The reason for A is unknown” or “We can’t solve practical problem B…”)
- [ ] What did the authors do? (“We measured/calculated/analysed X…”)
- [ ] How did the authors achieve this? (“We used method Y…”)
- [ ] What did the authors find out? (“We found out that X is…”)
- [ ] What does it all mean? (“This suggests that X is caused by…”, or “This provides a solution for B…”).
The introduction should be well referenced, and focus on relevant recent research rather than attempt an exhaustive analysis of the field.

# Intro

Key questions to ask about the introduction of a paper include:
- [ ] Does it give enough information for the journal’s readership to understand the context? (The audience for a specialist journal is likely to need less contextual information than that of a journal with a broader readership)
- [ ] Does it accurately describe the up-to-date research on the topic with properly-referenced statements and does it identify the limitations, controversies or knowledge gaps of those studies?
- [ ] Does it clearly explain why the study was necessary?
- [ ] Do the references cited adequately reflect the current state of the field? Is the reference citation balanced and fair (limited self-citation and citation of all relevant seminal studies)?

# Methods

When assessing whether the techniques have been employed properly, the following questions are useful:
- [ ] Is the methodology described in sufficient detail?
- [ ] Are there proper controls?
- [ ] Are there defined outcomes?
- [ ] Did they use validated instruments and/or techniques?
- [ ] Were experimental conditions acceptable?
- [ ] Are materials identified?
- [ ] Are the statistics sound and are any errors and uncertainties properly reported?
- [ ] Does the study allow for reproducibility?
- [ ] Was the study conducted objectively and without bias?
- [ ] Was the study conducted under ethical guidelines?


# Discussion/Conclusions
When reviewing the discussion, consider these questions:
- [ ] Is the conclusion consistent with the aims of the study?
- [ ] Does the conclusion answer the research question?
- [ ] Is the conclusion supported by the presented findings or are there alternative explanations for the results?
- [ ] Do the authors clearly explain the implications of the study for the field and its potential future applications? Do the authors discuss their results in the context of published studies?
